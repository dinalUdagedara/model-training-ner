# CrackInt Viva Preparation Guide (From Scratch)

Use this as your one-source checklist from now until viva day.

---

## 1) The Correct Path (What To Prepare First)

Follow this order exactly:

1. **Project story in 2 minutes**
   What problem you solved, why it matters, what you built, key results.
2. **Problem -> Gap -> Aim -> Objectives alignment**
   Show that each objective is achieved and evidenced.
3. **Methodology justification**
   Why pragmatism, abductive approach, mixed methods, and why that is valid for your project.
4. **Design and architecture decisions**
   Why this stack, why this architecture, why this model pipeline.
5. **Implementation depth**
   Demonstrate key modules, novelty vs adapted code, and technical trade-offs.
6. **Testing and evaluation evidence**
   Functional/non-functional tests, model metrics, evaluator feedback, limitations.
7. **Critical reflection**
   What did not work perfectly, what you learned, and what you would improve.
8. **Viva defense readiness**
   Prepare concise answers for technical, research, ethics, and decision-challenge questions.

If you master these 8 blocks, your viva will feel structured and controlled.

---

## 2) What Examiners Usually Test In Viva

They usually check 4 things:

- **Ownership**: Did you really do this work and understand every major decision?
- **Justification**: Can you explain _why_ you chose each method/tool, not just _what_ you used?
- **Evidence**: Can you prove claims with results, tables, screenshots, logs, or appendices?
- **Reflection**: Can you critically evaluate limitations and propose realistic future work?

## 3) Master Outline You Should Rehearse

Practice this exact flow:

## A. Project Overview (2-3 min)

- Project title and one-line purpose.
- Real-world problem (candidate tools are fragmented and generic).
- Proposed solution (unified AI interview preparation platform).
- Core features (resume/job extraction, personalized questions, semantic feedback, progress view).
- Outcome headline (usable prototype + NER metrics + evaluation findings).

## B. Research Foundation (4-5 min)

- Problem statement.
- Research gap from literature and existing platforms.
- Research questions and aims.
- Objectives and how they map to deliverables.

## C. Methodology (4-5 min)

- Research philosophy and approach.
- Why mixed methods (interviews + survey + competitive analysis + experiment).
- Requirement elicitation and triangulation logic.
- Risk management and ethics approach.

## D. Design and Technical Decisions (5-7 min)

- System architecture (frontend, backend, ML, storage, integrations).
- Data flow through key features.
- Why Next.js + FastAPI + PostgreSQL + AWS S3.
- Why Word2Vec + BiLSTM + CRF for NER in this context.
- Key trade-offs and constraints.

## E. Implementation (5-7 min)

- Core modules and responsibilities.
- Key API flows.
- Novel vs adapted vs third-party components.
- Security and reliability controls (auth, upload handling, configuration flags).

## F. Testing and Evaluation (6-8 min)

- Model testing setup and results.
- Functional and non-functional testing approach.
- Evaluation with users/peers/industry/academic feedback.
- Requirement coverage status and evidence.

## G. Critical Evaluation + Reflection (4-5 min)

- What worked well.
- Known limitations (model, data, UX, deployment realism, scope).
- Lessons learned (technical + project management + research).
- Future enhancements with priority and rationale.

## H. Close Strong (1 min)

- Re-state contribution.
- Re-state practical impact.
- Re-state what is novel and defensible.

## 5) High-Probability Viva Questions (Prepare Exact Answers)

### Project and research

- Why is this problem important now?
- What is your exact research gap?
- How is your work different from existing tools?

### Methodology

- Why did you choose this methodology and not an alternative?
- How did you ensure validity and reliability?
- How did requirements gathering influence your implementation?

### Technical design

- Why this architecture?
- Why this NER approach over transformer-based alternatives?
- What are the most critical technical trade-offs you made?

### Implementation and code ownership

- Which parts are fully your own work?
- What did you adapt and how did you verify correctness?
- Show one complex function and explain it line-by-line at a high level.

### Testing and evaluation

- Are your test metrics good enough? Why?
- What failed during testing and how did you respond?
- How do evaluation results validate your objectives?

### Ethics/security/professional

- What are key SLEP risks in your system?
- How do you handle privacy and sensitive data?
- What bias/fairness risks exist in AI-driven interview systems?

### Reflection

- If you had 3 more months, what would you improve first and why?
- What is the biggest limitation of your current prototype?
- What did you learn technically and academically?

## 6) Your 5-Answer Defense Formula (Use For Any Question)

When answering difficult questions, use:

1. **Position**: one-line direct answer.
2. **Reason**: why this was chosen.
3. **Evidence**: metric/table/chapter/appendix proof.
4. **Limitation**: honest boundary.
5. **Next step**: improvement plan.

This keeps answers confident, critical, and academic.

## 8) Red Flags To Avoid In Viva

- Over-describing features without justifying decisions.
- Claiming performance without citing exact evidence.
- Defensiveness when discussing limitations.
- Not distinguishing your own code from reused components.
- Saying "I don't know" without reflective follow-up.

Better alternative: "Within scope, this was the decision because X; limitation is Y; next improvement is Z."

## 11) Supervisor Mandatory Checklist (From Your Notes)

Treat this section as non-negotiable.

## A. Research gap and novelty

- Clearly define the research gap with evidence from at least 15 publications (2020+).
- Prioritize recent papers (especially 2024/2025) in your viva explanation.
- Explain the project difficulty level and why it is technically meaningful.
- Prepare one slide or one-page summary: "Existing solutions vs my gap vs my contribution".

## B. Skills growth (must be explicit)

- List new skills acquired beyond the syllabus (ML pipeline design, model training, deployment constraints, etc.).
- List existing skills improved (frontend architecture, backend API design, testing discipline, research writing).
- For each skill, prepare one concrete example from your implementation.

## C. UI/UX and reliability expectations

- Be ready to justify color scheme, readability, and UX flow decisions.
- Ensure demo path shows stable behavior with no crashes.
- Confirm all user interactions have validation and meaningful error handling.
- Confirm toast notifications are used instead of browser alert boxes.

## D. Testing expectations

- Functional testing evidence ready.
- Non-functional testing evidence ready.
- System-level testing evidence ready.
- Input validation examples prepared for key forms and API entry points.

## E. Code and thesis ownership

- Be able to explain source code confidently (frontend + backend + ML).
- Be able to explain thesis content chapter-by-chapter without reading.
- Prepare quick references to diagrams, tables, and appendices used as proof.

## F. ML viva deep-dive (very important)

- Explain both ML models line-by-line at high level (training and inference flow).
- Explain data pipeline from raw data to BIO tags to training splits.
- Justify datapoints and dataset size with multiple reasons (not only hardware constraints).
- Explain duplicate handling policy (if duplicates exist, why acceptable; if removed, why and how).
- Explain hyperparameters and why chosen (learning rate, hidden size, epochs, batch size, dropout, early stopping).
- Explain evaluation metrics clearly (precision, recall, F1, micro-F1, and their interpretation).
- Explain why 100% is not achieved and what contributes to residual error.
- Explain model architecture shape and algorithm logic in plain language.

## G. Frontend deep-dive

- Explain how data is fetched and synchronized in UI.
- Explain library choices (React Query / Axios / UI framework choices).
- Explain key hooks/state decisions and rendering flow.

## H. Backend deep-dive

- Explain API layer vs service/business layer boundaries.
- Explain key endpoint flow end-to-end.
- Explain SQLAlchemy ORM model relationships and persistence behavior.

## I. User interaction mastery

- Explain full user journey without live demo (screen-by-screen flow from memory).
- Explain validations and failure handling for every major user input path.
- Confirm no crash scenario in normal and edge-case flows.

## J. Thesis mastery tasks

- Read the full thesis end-to-end two times before viva.
- Create a chapter/subchapter skeleton note (headings only).
- Add bullet-point explanations under each heading (your own words).
- Prepare to explain any section when asked randomly.
- For each reference, know why it was included and what finding you used.
- Rehearse all major diagrams until explanation is fluent and concise.
